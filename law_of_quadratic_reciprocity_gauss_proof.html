Law of Quadratic Reciprocity (Gauss' Proof)

1. \( x^2 \equiv 3 \pmod{19} \) has integer solutions?

2. For which primes \( p \), \( x^2 \equiv 3 \pmod{p} \) has integer solutions?

(Legendre Symbol)
Let \( a \in \mathbb{Z} \) and \( p \) be a prime number. \[ \left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } p \mid a \\ 1 & \text{if } x^2 \equiv a \pmod{p} \text{ has an integer solution} \\ -1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

(Law of Quadratic Reciprocity)
Let $p,q$ be different odd primes. Then \begin{equation} \left( \frac{p}{q} \right) \left( \frac{q}{p} \right) = (-1)^{\frac{(p-1)(q-1)}{4}} \end{equation} with supplementary laws \begin{equation} \label{equ.suppl} \begin{split} \left( \frac{-1}{p} \right) &= (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}},\\ \left( \frac{2}{p} \right) &= (-1)^{\frac{p^2-1}{8}}, \end{split} \end{equation} 


Throughout the section $p$ is an odd prime and $a$ is an integer where $p\not | a$. Denote \begin{equation} \begin{split} R\colon &= \left\{ 1^2, 2^2, \cdots, \left( \frac{p-1}{2} \right)^2 \right \},\\ N \colon&= \{1, \cdots, p-1 \} \setminus R, \end{split} \end{equation} 

Exercise: $R$ is the complete quadratic residue set, and $N$ is the complete quadratic non-residue set in $\mod p$. 


Lemma 1.
Let \begin{equation} a R \colon = \left\{ a\, 1^2, \cdots, a \left(\frac{p-1}{2} \right)^2 \right\} \end{equation} then \begin{equation} a R = \left\{\begin{array}{cl}R & \text{if } \left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = 1 \\N & \text{if } \left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = -1\end{array}\right. \end{equation} 


If $\left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = 1$ then $ a \equiv x^2 \mod{p} $ for an integer $x$. Hence $a R = x^2 R \subset R$. Since $$ \# aR = \frac{p-1}{2} = \# R$$ we obtain $aR = R$.
If $\left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = -1$ then $aR \cap R = \emptyset$ because otherwise would imply $$ak^2 \equiv l^2 \mod{p} $$ for some $k,l \in \{1,\cdots,p-1\}$ hence $$a \equiv\left(\frac{k}{l}\right)^2 \equiv x^2 \mod{p} $$ for some integer $x$, which is a contradiction with $\left( \frac{a}{p} \right) =-1$. Thus $aR \subset N $. Since \begin{equation} \# aR = \frac{p-1}{2} = \# N \end{equation} we get $aR = N$ and the proof is complete. $\square$ 


Lemma 2. \begin{equation} \left( \frac{a}{p} \right) \equiv a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \mod{p} \end{equation} 


If $\left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = 1$, then $aR = R$ from Lemma 1. Hence, \begin{equation} \begin{split} \prod_{k=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} ak^2 &\equiv \prod_{l=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} l^2 \mod{p} \\ a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \left( \frac{p-1}{2} \right)!^2 &\equiv \left( \frac{p-1}{2} \right)!^2 \mod{p} \\ a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} &\equiv 1 \mod{p} \end{split} \end{equation}
If $\left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = -1$ then $aR = N$ and $\{1,\cdots,p-1\} = N \cup R = aR \cup R$. Hence, \begin{equation} \begin{split} (p-1)! &\equiv \prod_{k=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} a k^2 \prod_{l=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} l^2 \mod{p} \\ (p-1)! &\equiv a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \prod_{k=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} (p-k)^2 \prod_{l=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} l^2\mod{p} \\ &\equiv a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} (p-1)!^2 \mod{p} \end{split} \end{equation} and thus $a^{\frac{p-1}{2}}\equiv - 1 \mod{p} $ from Wilson's theorem ( $(p-1)! \equiv -1 \mod{p} $ ). $\square$ 


Let \begin{equation} S \colon = \left\{ 1, \cdots, \frac{p-1}{2} \right\} \end{equation} 

Lemma 3. (Gauss) Let \begin{equation} j \, S \colon = \left\{ j . 1, j.2, \cdots, j\left( \frac{p-1}{2} \right) \right\} \end{equation} for $j \in \mathbb{Z}$. If \begin{equation} \label{equ.v} v = \# a\,S \cap (-1)\,S \end{equation} then \begin{equation} \left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = (-1)^v \end{equation} 


For each $k \in S$, $$a k \equiv (-1)^{v_k} l \mod{p} $$ for a unique set of $(v_k, l )$ where $v_k\in\{0,1\}$ and $l \in S$. As $k$ spans through $S$ then so does $l$ because otherwise would imply $$a k_1^2 \equiv a k_2^2 \equiv l^2 \mod{p} $$ for some $k_1,k_2, l \in S$ with $k_1 \neq k_2$. But then $$a^2(k_1-k_2)(k_1+k_2) \equiv 0 \mod{p} $$ since $p\not | a$ and $2 \leq k_1+k_2 \leq p-1$ one obtains $k_1 = k_2$, which is a contradiction. Therefore, \begin{equation} \begin{split} \prod_{k \in S} ak &\equiv \prod_{k\in S}(-1)^{v_k} l \mod{p} \\ a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \prod_{k \in S} k &\equiv (-1)^{v_1+\cdots+v_{\frac{p-1}{2}}} \prod_{l \in S} l \mod{p} \\ a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} &\equiv (-1)^{v} \mod{p} \end{split} \end{equation} and from Lemma 2 one obtains $\left( \frac{a}{p} \right) = (-1)^{v}$. The proof is complete. $\square$ 


(Law of Quadratic reciprocity) We first prove the supplementary laws \eqref{equ.suppl}. When $a = -1$, evidently $$v = \# (-1) S \cap (-1) S = \frac{p-1}{2}$$ and Lemma 3 implies $\left( \frac{-1}{p} \right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}}$.
When $a = 2$, then \begin{equation} \begin{split} v &= \# \{2, 4, \cdots, p-1\} \cap \left\{-1, -2, \cdots, -\frac{(p-1)}{2} \right\} \\ &=\# \{-2, -4, \cdots, -p+1\} \cap \left\{1, 2, \cdots, \frac{p-1}{2} \right\} \\ &= \# \{1, 3, \cdots, p-2\} \cap\left\{1, 2, \cdots, \frac{p-1}{2} \right\}\\ & = \# \text{ of odd numbers in }\left \{1, 2, \cdots, \frac{p-1}{2}\right\} \end{split} \end{equation} Note that \begin{equation} \begin{split} p &= 8k+1 \to v = 2k,\\ p &= 8k+3 \to v=2k+1,\\ p &= 8k+5 \to v=2k+1,\\ p &= 8k+7 \to v=2k+2, \end{split} \end{equation} thus $$v\equiv \frac{p^2-1}{8}\mod{2}$$ and the supplementary laws are obtained.
Let \begin{equation} T \colon = \left \{ 1, \cdots, \frac{q-1}{2} \right \} \end{equation} and let $v_q$ is the number we obtain from \eqref{equ.v} when $a = q$. \begin{equation} \label{eq.vq} v_q = \# \left\{ (k,l) \colon -\frac{p}{2} < qk - pl < 0, k\in S, l \in T \right\} \end{equation} to see this notice that when $k$ is fixed \begin{equation} \label{eq.qkpl1} -\frac{p}{2} < qk - pl < 0 \end{equation} has at most one integer $l$ solution, and when it has one solution $qk - pl = -j$ for some $j \in S$ and \begin{equation} \label{eq.qkpl2} qk \equiv-j \mod{p} \end{equation} Similarly, when $k$ is fixed \eqref{eq.qkpl2} has at most one solution with $j\in S$ and in this case \eqref{eq.qkpl1} holds for some $l$. $l > 0$ as $k>0$. Moreover, $$pl = qk+j < q\frac{p}{2} + \frac{p}{2} = p \frac{q+1}{2}$$ hence $1\leq l \leq \frac{q-1}{2}$, i.e. $l \in T$. Thus, for a fixed $k$ $$qk \equiv-j \mod{p} \text{ has a solution }j\in S \iff -\frac{p}{2} < qk - pl < 0 \text{ has a solution }l \in T$$ (through the relation $ l = \frac{pl + j}{q}$). As $k$ spans through $S$ \eqref{eq.vq} follows immediately and the claim is proved.
When we switch $p$ and $q$ we obtain $$v_p = \# \left\{ (k,l) \colon -\frac{q}{2} < pl - qk < 0, k\in S, l \in T \right\} $$ hence $v_p + v_q$ is the number of solutions to \begin{equation} \label{eq.sol1} -\frac{p}{2} < qk - pl < \frac{q}{2} \end{equation} with $k \in S$ and $l\in T$. There are $\frac{(p-1)}{2}\times\frac{(q-1)}{2}$ possibilities for $(k,l)$ and we show that the number of non solutions to \eqref{eq.sol1}, which is $\frac{(p-1)(q-1)}{4} - (v_p + v_q)$, is even. To see this, note that $(k,l)$ is a solution to \eqref{eq.sol1} if and only if $$-\frac{(q+p)}{4} < [ qk - pl ] - \frac{q-p}{4} < \frac{(q+p)}{4}$$ that is if and only if \begin{equation} \label{eq.sol2} \left| [ qk - pl ] - \frac{q-p}{4} \right| < \frac{q+p}{4} \end{equation} When we set \begin{equation} \begin{split} K &= \frac{p-1}{2} - k, \\ L &= \frac{q-1}{2} - l, \end{split} \end{equation} \eqref{eq.sol2} implies \begin{equation} \begin{split} \left| [ qK - pL ] - \frac{q-p}{4} \right| &= \left| q\left( \frac{p-1}{2} - k \right) - p\left( \frac{q-1}{2} - l \right) - \frac{q-p}{4} \right| \\ &= \left| \frac{q-p}{4} - [ qk - pl ] \right| \end{split} \end{equation} Thus, $(k,l)$ is a non solution to \eqref{eq.sol2} if and only if $(K,L)$ is a non solution to \eqref{eq.sol2}.
When $(k,l)$ is a non solution, then $(K,L)$ must be different than $(k,l)$ since otherwise would imply $$k=K = \frac{p-1}{4}~,~l=L=\frac{q-1}{4}$$ which is indeed a solution to \eqref{eq.sol2}. Therefore, when we pair up $(k,l)$ with $(K,L)$ for all possible $k,l$ we obtain that the number of non solutions to \eqref{eq.sol2} are even, and thus \begin{equation} \frac{(p-1)(q-1)}{4} \equiv v_p + v_q \mod{2} \end{equation} and this and Lemma 3 immediately imply \begin{equation} \begin{split} (-1)^{\frac{(p-1)(q-1)}{4} } &= (-1)^{ v_p + v_q} \\ &= \left( \frac{p}{q} \right) \left( \frac{q}{p} \right) \end{split} \end{equation} the proof is complete. $\square$ 


The following results are immediate:


(i) $x^2 \equiv -1 \mod{p} $ has integer solutions iff $p\equiv 1\mod{4}$ 


(ii) $x^2 \equiv 2 \mod{p} $ has integer solutions iff $p \equiv \pm 1 \mod{8}$ 


We want to find all prime $p$ such that $\left( \frac{3}{p} \right)=1$. This holds when $p=2$. We may assume $p$ is odd. The Law of Quadratic Reciprocity implies $$\left( \frac{3}{p} \right) \left( \frac{p}{3} \right) = (-1)^{\frac{2(p-1)}{4}} = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}}$$ and hence \begin{equation} \label{eq.3p} \left( \frac{3}{p} \right)=1 \iff \left( \frac{p}{3} \right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \end{equation} when $p\equiv 1(\mod 4)$; \eqref{eq.3p} implies $\left( \frac{p}{3} \right) = 1$ which is $p \equiv 1 \mod{3}$ and when $p\equiv 3(\mod 4)$; \eqref{eq.3p} implies $\left( \frac{p}{3} \right) = -1$, i.e. $p \equiv 2\mod{3}$. Therefore, $$\left( \frac{3}{p} \right) = 1 \iff p=2 \text{ or }p\equiv\pm1 \mod{12} $$ 



Let $a,b \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $p \not | ab$. Show that $\left( \frac{a b}{p} \right) = \left( \frac{a}{p} \right) \left( \frac{b}{p} \right) $ 


If both in $R$: $a\equiv x^2\mod{p} $ and $b\equiv y^2\mod{p} $, hence $ab \equiv (xy)^2\mod{p} $, and hence $\left(\frac{ab}{p}\right) =1$.
If one in $R$ one in $N$: say $a\in N$ and $b\in R$. Lemma 1 implies $aR = N$, and since $ab \in aR = N$, we get $\left(\frac{ab}{p}\right) =-1$.
If both in $N$: Lemma 1 implies $aR = N$, and since $b\in N$ there is an integer $k$ where $ak^2\equiv b\mod{p} $. Hence, $ab \equiv a^2k^2 \equiv (ak)^2\mod{p} $, and hence $\left(\frac{ab}{p}\right) =1$.



Find all primes $p$ for which $x^2 \equiv -2 \mod{p} $ has integer solution. 


We want to find primes $p$ for which $\left( \frac{-2}{p} \right)=1$. From previous result and supplementary laws we have $$\left( \frac{-2}{p} \right) = \left( \frac{-1}{p} \right)\left( \frac{2}{p} \right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2} + \frac{p^2-1}{8}} = (-1)^{\frac{(p-1)(p+5)}{8}}$$ Thus, $$ \left( \frac{-2}{p} \right)=1 \iff p \equiv 1,3 \mod{8} $$



Find all primes $p$ for which $x^2 \equiv 15 \mod{p} $ has integer solution. 


We want to find primes $p$ for which $\left( \frac{15}{p} \right)=1 =\left( \frac{3}{p} \right) \left( \frac{5}{p} \right) $. Law of reciprocity implies $$\left( \frac{3}{p} \right) \left( \frac{p}{3} \right)=(-1)^{\frac{2(p-1)}{4}} = (-1)^{\frac{(p-1)}{2}} \text{ and } \left( \frac{5}{p} \right) \left( \frac{p}{5} \right)=(-1)^{\frac{4(p-1)}{4}} = 1 $$ hence $$ \left( \frac{3}{p} \right) \left( \frac{p}{3} \right) \left( \frac{5}{p} \right) \left( \frac{p}{5} \right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} $$ and hence $$ \left( \frac{3}{p} \right) \left( \frac{5}{p} \right) =1 \iff \left( \frac{p}{3} \right)\left( \frac{p}{5} \right)= (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}}$$
(a) when $p\equiv 1 \mod{ 4}$: $ \left( \frac{p}{3} \right)\left( \frac{p}{5} \right) = 1 $, hence
(a.1) $p\equiv 1 \mod{3} \to p\equiv 1,4 \mod{5}$ or
(a.2) $p\equiv 2 \mod{3} \to p\equiv 2,3\mod{5}$

(b) when $p\equiv 3 \mod{4}$: $ \left( \frac{p}{3} \right)\left( \frac{p}{5} \right) = -1 $, hence
(b.1) $p\equiv 1 \mod{3} \to p\equiv 2,3\mod{5}$ or
(b.2) $p\equiv 2 \mod{3} \to p\equiv 1,4 \mod{5}$

and when all combined, we obtain $$ \left( \frac{15}{p} \right)=1 \iff p \equiv 1, 7, 11, 17, 43, 49,53,59 \mod{60} $$


Let $p\equiv 1 \mod{4}$. Prove that for infinitely many primes $q$ the equation $$x^2 \equiv p \mod{ q}$$ has no integer solution.

Assume the contrary. Let $q_1,\cdots,q_k$ be all primes for which $$\left( \frac{p}{q_j} \right) = -1$$ Choose $a\in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $\left( \frac{a}{p}\right)=-1$. We use Chinese Remainder theorem and choose an integer $n > 0$ such that \begin{equation} \begin{split} n & \equiv a \mod{p} \\ n & \neq 0 \mod{q_j} \text{ for }j=1,\cdots,k \end{split} \end{equation} Let $n = r_1 r_2 \cdots r_k$ where $r_i$ are primes (not necessarily distinct). On the one hand \begin{equation} \left( \frac{p}{r_i} \right) = 1 \end{equation} for $i$ since $r_i \notin \{q_1,\cdots,q_k\}$. On the other hand LQR and $p\equiv 1 \mod{4}$ imply \begin{equation} \left(\frac{p}{r_i} \right)\left(\frac{r_i}{p} \right) = 1 \end{equation} hence $\left(\frac{r_i}{p} \right) = 1$ for all $i=1,\cdots, l$. Exercise 2 implies $$ 1 =\left(\frac{r_1}{p} \right)\cdots \left(\frac{r_l}{p} \right) = \left(\frac{n}{p} \right) $$ which contradicts with $\left(\frac{n}{p} \right) = \left(\frac{a}{p} \right) =-1 $. Hence, there are infinitely many primes $q$ for which $x^2\equiv p \mod{q}$ has no integer solutions.
