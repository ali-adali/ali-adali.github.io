Linear independence of square roots over integers

Ali ADALI

30 June, 2020


Abstract

An elementary proof is presented for the linear independence of $\sqrt{n_1}, \cdots, \sqrt{n_k}$ over the integers, where $n_1, \cdots, n_k$ are distinct square-free positive integers.

Let $n_1, \cdots, n_k$ be relatively prime odd positive integers. First, I prove the following result:

Lemma 1.

Let $\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)$ denote the Ledenre symbol: \begin{equation} \left(\frac{a}{b}\right) \text{ is } 1 \text{ if }x^2\equiv a \pmod{b} \text{ has an integer solution and} -1 \text{ otherwise} \end{equation} There exists an integer $m$ such that $\left(\frac{n_1}{m} \right) = -1$ and $\left(\frac{n_1}{m} \right) = 1$ for each $i \geq 2$.

Proof.

Let's choose $m$ such that $m$ is relatively prime with $n_i$ for all $i\geq 1$ and $m\equiv 1 \pmod{4}$. Then, the generalized Jacobi's Reciprocity Law (Landau [1] theorem 94) implies \begin{equation} \label{equ.1} \left(\frac{m}{n_i}\right) \left(\frac{n_i}{m}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{(m-1)(n_i-1)}{2}} = 1. \end{equation} Since $n_i$ are relatively prime it is possible by Chinese remainder theorem to choose $m$ such that it equals to a non-square value in modulo $n_1$ and a square value in modulo $n_i$ for all $i \geq 2$. This choice of $m$ satisfies the condition of the Lemma 1 due to \eqref{equ.1} and the proof is complete. $\square$


Next, I prove that $m$ in Lemma 1 can be chosen as a prime .

Lemma 2.

There exists a prime number $p$ such that $\left(\frac{n_1}{p} \right) = -1$ and $\left(\frac{n_i}{p} \right) = 1$ for each $i \geq 2$.


Proof.

Note that Dirichlet's theorem about primes in arithmetic progression will immediately imply the result. However, we don't really need such a strong result and we can obtain a proof using elementary methods. First, we prove the following fact: when $n$ is an odd integer and $q$ is an odd prime which is not a divisor of $n$, then \begin{equation} \label{eq.nq1} \left( \frac{n}{q} \right) = \left( \frac{n}{q^k} \right) \end{equation} for every $k \geq 1$. Note that $\left( \frac{n}{q} \right) = -1 \implies \left( \frac{n}{q^k} \right) =-1$ is straightforward. We prove the rest by induction: Let $\left( \frac{n}{q} \right) = 1$. Let the assertion holds for $k=1,\cdots,N$. Let $$n \equiv x_N^2\pmod{q^{N}}$$ and let $$x_N^2 = n + q^N l_N$$ for some integer $l_N$. Since $n$ and $q$ are odd, one can choose an integer $s_N$ such that $$2x_N s_N + l_N \equiv 0 \pmod{q}.$$ Choose $x_{N+1} = x_N + q^N s_N$, then \begin{equation} \begin{split} x_{N+1}^2 &= (x_N + q^N s_N)^2 \\ &= x_N^2 +2x_N s_N q^N + s_N^2 q^{2N} \\ &= n + q^N l_N + 2x_N s_N q^N + s_N^2 q^{2N} \\ &= n + q^{N}( 2x_N s_N + l_N ) +s_N^2 q^{2N} \\ & \equiv n \pmod{q^{N+1}}. \end{split} \end{equation} Hence $\left( \frac{n}{q^{N+1}} \right) = 1$ and the assertion is proved for $k=N+1$. By induction, it must hold for all positive integers $k$. Now, use Lemma 1 to choose an $m$ for $n_1, \cdots, n_k$. Let $m = p_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots p_{r}^{\alpha_r}$. \eqref{eq.nq1} and the property $\left( \frac{n}{a b} \right) = 1 \iff \left( \frac{n}{a} \right) = 1 $ and $\left( \frac{n}{ b} \right) =1$ when $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime imply $$ \left( \frac{n_1}{m} \right) = -1 \implies \left( \frac{n_1}{p_j^{\alpha_j}} \right) = -1 \iff \left( \frac{n_1}{p_j} \right) = -1.$$ Moreover, $\left( \frac{n_i}{m} \right) = 1 \implies \left( \frac{n_i}{p_j} \right) = 1$ is straightforward since $p_j | m$. Hence choosing $p = p_j$ works and the proof is complete. $\square$


We introduce a norm-like product which will come in handy when relating the sums of square root of integers with arithmetic in modulo $p$.


Lemma 3.

Let \begin{equation} Q(x_1, \cdots, x_l) \colon = \prod_{j_1,\cdots,j_l \in \{0,1\}}{\left( \sum_{i=1}^{l}{(-1)^{j_i}x_i} \right)} \end{equation} Then $Q(x_1,\cdots,x_l) = P(x_1^2,\cdots,x_l^2)$ where $P$ is a $l$-variable polynomial over integers.


Proof.

Note that \begin{equation} Q = \prod_{d}(x_1 + d )(x_1 - d) (-x_1+d)(-x_1-d) = \prod_{d}(x_1^2 - d^2)^2 \end{equation} where $d$'s are $x_1$-free. Hence, only the even powers of $x_1$ appear in $Q$. Due to symmetry, this is true for $x_2, \cdots x_l$ as well and the proof is complete. $\square$

Theorem 4.

Let $n_1, \cdots, n_k$ be relatively prime square free odd positive integers. Let $a_1,\cdots, a_k$ be integers. If the sum \begin{equation} a_1\sqrt{n_1}+\cdots + a_k\sqrt{n_k} \end{equation} is an integer then $a_1 =\cdots=a_k = 0$.


Proof.

Let $$a_1\sqrt{n_1} + \cdots+a_k\sqrt{n_k} + c = 0$$ for some integers $a_1,\cdots,a_k$ and $c$. Assume the contrary that not all $a_i$'s are zero. Then without loss of generality we may assume that $a_1 \neq 0$. Let $p$ be a prime as in Lemma 2: $$\left(\frac{n_1}{p} \right) = -1$$ and $$\left(\frac{n_i}{p} \right) = 1$$ for $i\geq 2$. Let $n_i \equiv t_i^2(\mod p)$ for $i\geq 2$. We also demand that $p$ does not divide $a_1$. ( Otherwise set $n_{k+1} =p$ and apply the lemma with $n_1, \cdots, n_{k+1}$ to obtain a new prime. Continue this process until you reach a prime $p$ which doesn't divide $a_1$) Since $a_1\sqrt{n_1} + \cdots+a_k\sqrt{n_k} + c$ is a factor of $Q( a_1\sqrt{n_1}, \cdots, a_k\sqrt{n_k}, c )$, Lemma 3 implies \begin{equation} \begin{split} 0 &= Q( a_1\sqrt{n_1}, \cdots, a_k\sqrt{n_k}, c ) \\ &= P( a_1^2 n_1, \cdots, a_k^2 n_k, c^2 )\\ &\equiv P( a_1^2 n_1, a_2^2t_2^2 \cdots, a_k^2 t_k^2, c^2 ) \\ &\equiv Q( a_1\sqrt{n_1},a_2t_2, \cdots, a_kt_k, c ) \\ &\equiv \prod_{d}(a_1^2 n_1 - d^2)^2 \pmod{p}. \end{split} \end{equation} where $d$'s are all integers as they are linear combinations of $a_2t_2, \cdots, a_kt_k$ and $c$ over integers. Hence, all the terms of the RHS of the last equation are integers. Since the product is $0$ modulo $p$, then at least one $d$ we must have $$a_1^2 n_1 - d^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}.$$ Since $p$ does not divide $a_1$ one has $$n_1 \equiv \left(\frac{d}{a_1}\right)^2 \pmod{p}$$ and this contradicts with $\left(\frac{n_1}{p} \right) = -1$. Hence $a_1=0$, and the proof is complete. $\square$


Theorem 5.

Let $n_1, \cdots, n_k > 1$ be distinct square free integers. If the sum \begin{equation} a_1 \sqrt{n_1} + \cdots +a_{k}\sqrt{n_k} \end{equation} is an integer for some integers $a_1,\cdots, a_k$, then $a_1 = \cdots = a_k = 0$.

Proof.

We prove the claim by induction. When $k=1$, if $$a_1\sqrt{n_1} = c$$ for some square free integer $n_1 >1$ and some integer $c$, then $$a_1^2 n_1 = c^2.$$ Let $p_1$ be a prime divisor of $n_1$. Then the power $p_1$ in $c^2$ is even where it is odd in $a_1^2 n_1$ unless $a_1$ is zero. Hence, $a_1=0$ and the claim holds. Assume that the claim holds for $k=1,\cdots, K$. Let $n_1, \cdots,n_{K+1} > 1$ be square free integers and let \begin{equation} \label{eq.sumai} \sum_{i=1}^{K+1}{a_i \sqrt{n_i}} = c \end{equation} for some integers $a_1, \cdots, a_{K+1}$ and $c$. Since $n_i$ are distinct and we have at least two, at least one of them must have an odd prime divisor, say $q$. Without loss of generality let $q$ be a divisor of $n_1, \cdots, n_l$ and $q$ is relatively prime with $n_{l+1}, \cdots, n_{K+1}$ where $l \geq 1$. Let $$n_i = q \tilde{n}_i$$ for $i=1,\cdots,l$. Note that $q$ is relatively prime with $\tilde{n}_i$ for $i = 1,\cdots,l$ since $n_i$ is square free. Let $d$ be an integer such that $$\left( \frac{d}{q} \right)=-1.$$ Choose $m$ as \begin{equation} \label{eq.m} \begin{split} m &\equiv 1 \pmod{4 \prod_{i=1}^{l}\tilde{n}_i \prod_{i=l+1}^{K+1}n_i},\\ m &\equiv d \pmod{q}. \end{split} \end{equation} First, note that $\left(\frac{m}{\tilde{n}_i} \right) = 1$ and $\left(\frac{m}{n_i}\right)=1$ for $i=1,\cdots,K+1$ and $\left(\frac{m}{q} \right) = -1$. Generalized reciprocity law implies \begin{equation} \begin{split} \left(\frac{m}{T}\right)\left( \frac{T}{m}\right) &= (-1)^{\frac{(m-1)(T-1)}{4}} = 1,\\ \left( \frac{2}{m}\right) &= (-1)^{\frac{m^2-1}{8}} = 1, \end{split} \end{equation} for odd $T$, thus \begin{equation} \begin{split} \label{eq.tilden} \left(\frac{\tilde{n}_i}{m} \right) &= \left(\frac{m}{\tilde{n}_i} \right) = 1,\\ \left(\frac{n_i}{m}\right)&=\left(\frac{m}{n_i}\right)=1,\\ \left(\frac{q}{m} \right) &= \left(\frac{m}{q} \right) = -1. \end{split} \end{equation} Let $p$ be an odd prime divisor of $n$. Then, evidently \begin{equation} \begin{split} \left(\frac{\tilde{n}_i}{p} \right) &= 1,\\ \left(\frac{n_i}{p}\right)&=1,\\ \left(\frac{q}{p} \right) &= -1, \end{split} \end{equation} for $i=1,\cdots,K+1$. Let $\tilde{n}_i \equiv \tilde{x}_i^2 \pmod{p}$ and $n_i\equiv x_i^2 \pmod{p}$. Since $$\sum_{i=1}^{K+1}{a_i\sqrt{n_i}} - c = 0$$ is a divisor of $Q(a_1\sqrt{n_1},\cdots, a_{K+1}\sqrt{K+1},-c)$, one has \begin{equation} \begin{split} 0 &= Q(a_1\sqrt{n_1},\cdots, a_{K+1}\sqrt{n_{K+1}},-c) \\ &= P(a_1^2 n_1,\cdots, a_{K+1}^2 n_{K+1},c^2) \\ &\equiv P(a_1^2 \tilde{x_1}^2 q,\cdots,a_l^2 \tilde{x_l}^2 q, a_{l+1}^2 x_{l+1}^2,\cdots , a_{K+1}^2 x_{K+1}^2,c^2)\\ &\equiv Q(a_1 \tilde{x_1} \sqrt{q},\cdots,a_l \tilde{x_l} \sqrt{q}, a_{l+1} x_{l+1},\cdots , a_{K+1} x_{K+1},c) \\ &\equiv \prod_{b,d}( b \sqrt{q} + d ) (b \sqrt{q} - d )( -b \sqrt{q} + d )( b \sqrt{q} - d )\\ &\equiv \prod_{b,d}(b^2 q - d^2)^2 \pmod{p} \end{split} \end{equation} where $b$ runs over all the sums of the form $\sum_{i=1}^{l}{(-1)^{j_i}a_i \tilde{x}_i}$ and $d$ runs over all the sums of the form $\sum_{i=l+1}^{K+1}{(-1)^{j_i}a_i x_i}$ where $j_i \in\{0,1\}$ for $i=1\cdots, K+1$. Since all the quantities on the right hand side are integers, we must have $$b^2 q - d^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$$ for at least one couple of $(b,d)$. Since $\left( \frac{q}{p} \right)=-1$, we must have $$b \equiv 0 \pmod{q}.$$ Note that $b$ is a divisor of $Q(a_1\tilde{x}_1,\cdots,a_l\tilde{x}_l)$, hence $$Q(a_1\tilde{x}_1,\cdots,a_l \tilde{x}_l) \equiv 0 \pmod{p}.$$ This is on one hand, on the other hand \begin{equation} \label{eqQ} \begin{split} 0 &\equiv Q(a_1\tilde{x}_1,\cdots,a_l \tilde{x}_l)\\ &= P(a_1^2\tilde{x}_1^2,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{x}_l^2) \\ &\equiv P(a_1^2\tilde{n}_1,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{n}_l) \pmod{p} \end{split} \end{equation} implying that \begin{equation} p | m \implies p | P(a_1^2\tilde{n}_1,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{n}_l), \end{equation} where $P(a_1^2\tilde{n}_1,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{n}_l)$ a constant integer. Observe that the set of $m$ satisfying \eqref{eq.m} constitutes and arithmetic sequence. An arithmetic sequence of integer numbers have infinitely many distinct prime divisors due to the fact that $At + B$ will have always a different prime divisor than any set of primes $\{r_1, \cdots, r_s\}$ when substituted $k = Br_1\cdots r_s$. Hence, we can choose $p$ as large as we want, in particular, we choose $$ p > \left|P(a_1^2\tilde{n}_1,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{n}_l) \right|.$$ Hence, \eqref{eqQ} implies $$P(a_1^2\tilde{n}_1,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{n}_l) = 0$$ identically. Note that $$ 0 = P(a_1^2\tilde{n}_1,\cdots,a_l^2\tilde{n}_l) =Q(a_1\sqrt{\tilde{n}_1},\cdots,a_l\sqrt{\tilde{n}_l}),$$ hence \begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^{l} a_i' \sqrt{\tilde{n}_i} = 0 \end{equation} where $a_i' = (-1)^{j_i} a_i$ for some $j_1, \cdots, j_l$ where $j_i \in \{0,1\}$ for $i=1,\cdots, l$. Multiplying by $\sqrt{\tilde{n}_1}$ yields \begin{equation} \label{eq.l1} \sum_{i=2}^{l} a_i' \sqrt{\tilde{n_1} \tilde{n}_i } = - a_1' \tilde{n} \end{equation} since $n_i$ are distinct $\sqrt{\tilde{n_1}\tilde{n_i}}$ are never integers. Since $l-1 \leq K$, \eqref{eq.l1} and hypothesis for $k=l-1$ imply $a_i' = 0$ for $i=2,\cdots,l$ and consequently for $a_1'$. This implies $$a_1=\cdots=a_l = 0.$$ Substituting this into \eqref{eq.sumai} yields \begin{equation} \label{eq.l2} \sum_{i=l+1}^{K+1}{a_i \sqrt{n_i}} = c \end{equation} Since $l\geq 1$, hypothesis for $k=K-l$ implies $a_i = 0$ for $i=l+1\cdots K+1$ in \eqref{eq.l2}. Hence, hypothesis hold for $k=K+1$, and by induction it must hold for all positive integers. The proof is complete. $\square$


Corollary 6.

Let $n_1,\cdots,n_k$ be positive integers and $a_1,\cdots, a_k$ be integers. If the sum \begin{equation} a_1\sqrt{n_1} + \cdots + a_{k}\sqrt{n_k} \end{equation} is a non zero integer, then $n_i$ is a square for all $i=1,\cdots, k$.


We can use these results to prove the following lovely property

Theorem 7.

A rectangle $R$ is partitioned into smaller rectangles $R_1, \cdots, R_n$. If all the dimensions of the smaller rectangles are square root of some integers and if the area of $R$ is a prime number, then all dimensions of all rectangles are integers.

Proof.

The dimensions of $R$ must be of the form $\sum_{i=1}^{N}{a_i\sqrt{n_i}}$ and $\sum_{j}^{M}{b_j\sqrt{m_j}}$ where $a_i$, $b_j$ are positive integers and $n_i$, $m_j$ are square free integers for $i=1,\cdots,N$ and $j=1,\cdots, M$. Let $p$ be the area of $R$ then \begin{equation} \label{eq.aibj} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{M}{ a_i b_j \sqrt{n_i m_j} } = p, \end{equation} Corollary 1 implies $n_i m_j$ is square for all $i=1,\cdots,N$ and $j=1\cdots, M$. Observe that the product of two square free integers is a square if and only if two numbers are equal, hence $$K = n_i = m_j $$ for $i=1,\cdots,N$ and $j=1,\cdots, M$. In this case, $$\sqrt{n_i m_j} = K,$$ and thus the left hand side of \eqref{eq.aibj} is a multiple of $K$. $p$ is a multiple of $K$, hence either $K=p$, which is possible only when there is only one rectangle in the partition, or $K=1$. In both cases, all dimensions have to be integers. $\square$
